History

1961 The Supreme Court, under Chief Justice Earl Warren, unanimously upheld the constitutionality of a jury selection system that discriminated against women on the grounds that "women are at the center of home and family life." The observation reflected dominant social values at the time, but the Court was unable then to see how such values thwarted the promise of equality for women implicit in the Constitution.
1963 Equal Pay Act
1964 Title VII of the Civil Rights Act barred some forms of sex discrimination.
1971 Reed v. Reed - For the first time, the Court held that a classification based on sex was unconstitutional, in violation of the equal protection clause.
1975 Secured its first major grant from the Ford Foundation which enabled us to develop a significant program to enforce women's statutory rights, including the rights to equal employment opportunity guaranteed under Title VII and the Equal Pay Act.
1976 Craig v. Boren - the Supreme Court established the "heightened scrutiny" standard for measuring the constitutionality of sex-based classifications.
1977 Califano v. Goldfarb, in which the Supreme Court struck down discriminatory Social Security regulations, finding that they were the "accidental by-product of (legislators') traditional way of thinking about females".
